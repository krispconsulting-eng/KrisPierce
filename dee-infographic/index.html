Developmental & Epileptic Encephalopathies (DEE)

One diagnosis — multiple disabilities at once, reaching across every body system and every stage of life.

DEE is a group of severe, early-onset epilepsies in which frequent epileptiform brain activity itself drives developmental slowing and regression. The result is rarely a single impairment — it is a web of co-occurring disabilities that evolve and compound over a lifetime.

Disability across body systems

DEE rarely affects one area in isolation. In the same person, impairments appear simultaneously across the brain, the body, the senses, vital organs, and behaviour — each one amplifying the others.

Co-occurring, not isolated. Most people with DEE live with several of these disabilities at the same time — the reason care is so complex.

Cognitive & Intellectual

The developing brain
  • Intellectual disability in nearly all individuals — moderate to profound.
  • Global developmental delay across skills and milestones.
  • Cognitive skills may plateau or actively regress from ongoing epileptiform activity.

Motor & Physical

Movement & bones
  • Movement disorders: spasticity, tremor, dyskinesia, dystonia.
  • Orthopaedic complications from abnormal tone & limited mobility — scoliosis, hip dislocation.
  • Bone fragility & fracture risk from anti-seizure medication and reduced weight-bearing.

Communication & Sensory

Speech & vision
  • Expressive & receptive speech heavily impacted; some never speak and rely on non-verbal / AAC methods.
  • Cortical Vision Impairment (CVI) is a common co-occurring condition.

Medical & Systemic

Vital & life-threatening
  • Gastrointestinal: dysphagia & reflux (GERD) raise aspiration & pneumonia risk — feeding tubes often required.
  • Sleep dysregulation: night-time wakefulness & daytime sleepiness limit daily participation.
  • Life-threatening risk: severe seizures, status epilepticus, and SUDEP.

Behavioural & Psychiatric

Mood & neurodevelopment
  • Frequent overlap with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) and ADHD.
  • Chronic behavioural problems, mood disturbance & psychiatric issues complicate daily care and education.

The compounding effect

Why it matters
  • A seizure isn't one event — it can set back cognition, motor control and sleep all at once.
  • Disabilities interact: poor sleep worsens seizures; low mobility worsens bones; vision loss limits communication.
  • Care must be coordinated across many specialists, for life.

Disability across the lifespan

The impact does not pass with childhood. Disabilities emerge in infancy, deepen and accumulate through development, and require lifelong, intensive support — under a constant, heightened risk to life.

Accumulating toll

Adulthood

Lifelong support

  • Multi-system management ongoing
  • Persistent seizure & SUDEP risk
