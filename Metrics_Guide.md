# Where to Find Your Publication, Engagement and Impact Metrics

**Prepared by Kristine Pierce**

Record each metric with three pieces of information: **the number**, **the source platform**, and **the date of retrieval**. A citation count without a source and date is unverifiable, and grant panels expect all three.

**Note:** Counts differ between platforms. Google Scholar returns higher numbers than Scopus or Web of Science because it indexes grey literature, theses, and preprints. Always state which platform you used.

---

## 1. Peer-Reviewed Publications

**What to report:** Total peer-reviewed journal articles, book chapters, and conference papers. Report separately if required.

**Where to find it:**

- **Google Scholar:** [scholar.google.com](https://scholar.google.com). Search your name, click your profile. The number listed under your name is your total publication count. Includes non-indexed journals and preprints.
- **Scopus:** [scopus.com](https://www.scopus.com). Authors tab, search your name, select your profile, Documents tab. More conservative than Google Scholar.
- **Web of Science:** [webofscience.com](https://www.webofscience.com). In Author Search, enter your name; the Results tab shows the total publications in the WoS core collection.

**Record as:** *'12 peer-reviewed publications (Google Scholar, June 2026)'*

---

## 2. Q1 Publications

**What to report:** Number and percentage of publications in journals ranked in the top 25% (Q1) by CiteScore or SJR. Q1 status is journal-level, not article-level, and is assessed by publication year.

**Where to find it:**

- **SciVal (recommended):** [scival.com](https://www.scival.com), institutional login required. Researchers, search your name, Outputs, filter by CiteScore Percentile, select Top 25%. Count the outputs shown, divide by total publications for your Q1 percentage.
- **Scimago Journal Rank (free):** [scimagojr.com](https://www.scimagojr.com). Journal Search, enter each journal title, select the year of your publication, and check the SJR Best Quartile. Time-consuming for large lists but free.
- **Scopus Source List:** [scopus.com](https://www.scopus.com), Sources tab, search journal title. CiteScore Tracker shows current and historical quartile ranking.

**Record as:** *'8 of 12 publications (67%) in Q1 journals (SciVal, June 2026)'*

---

## 3. Total Citations

**What to report:** Total number of times your published work has been cited. Report all-time citations and, if relevant, citations in the last 5 years.

**Where to find it:**

- **Google Scholar (most comprehensive):** [scholar.google.com](https://scholar.google.com), your profile. The Citations column shows all-time and since-year totals. Click any paper to see who has cited it.
- **Scopus:** [scopus.com](https://www.scopus.com), Authors, your profile, Citation overview tab. Shows citations by year, total, and excludes self-citations if needed.
- **Web of Science:** [webofscience.com](https://www.webofscience.com), Author Search, your profile, Citation Report. Total times cited shown at top.

**Record as:** *'342 citations (Google Scholar, June 2026)'* or *'198 citations excluding self-citations (Scopus, June 2026)'*

---

## 4. h-index

**What to report:** Your h-index, where h equals the number of papers you have published that have each been cited at least h times. An h-index of 15 means 15 papers each cited 15 or more times.

**Where to find it:**

- **Google Scholar:** [scholar.google.com](https://scholar.google.com), your profile. Displayed directly beneath your total citations. Shows all-time and since-year h-index. The most widely used and most inclusive count.
- **Scopus:** [scopus.com](https://www.scopus.com), Authors, your profile. h-index shown on the summary page. Based on Scopus-indexed publications only.
- **Web of Science:** [webofscience.com](https://www.webofscience.com), Author Search, Citation Report. h-index shown at the top of the report.

**Record as:** *'h-index 14 (Google Scholar, June 2026)'*. Google Scholar h-index is almost always higher than Scopus; always state which platform you used.

## 5. Media Engagements

**What to report:** Media appearances or mentions across TV, radio, print, and digital outlets. Include outlet name, date, format, and whether you were interviewed or cited.

**Where to find it:**

- **Altmetric Explorer:** [altmetric.com/explorer](https://www.altmetric.com/explorer), institutional access. Search your name or a paper DOI, click the Altmetric doughnut, News tab shows print and digital pickups, Blogs tab shows online coverage. Export to CSV for a full list.
- **PlumX Metrics (via Scopus):** [scopus.com](https://www.scopus.com), search your paper, PlumX Metrics panel on the right, Usage, News. Repeat across your key outputs.
- **Google Alerts (free):** [google.com/alerts](https://www.google.com/alerts). Set alerts for your full name, program name, and key paper titles. Google emails you when these appear in news sources. Log each result with date, outlet, and format.
- **Meltwater / Factiva:** Institutional media monitoring services that capture broadcast (TV/radio) alongside print and digital. Altmetric does not reliably capture broadcast; these services do. Contact your institution's communications team for access.

**Record as:** *'15 media mentions: 3 TV, 4 radio, 5 print, 3 digital (Altmetric, June 2026)'*. For broadcast not captured by Altmetric, keep your own log with outlet, date, and format.

## 6. Policy Citations

**What to report:** Number of times your work has been cited in policy documents: government reports, parliamentary submissions, clinical guidelines, WHO or UN documents, and NGO policy briefs.

**Where to find it:**

- **Overton (recommended, most comprehensive):** [overton.io](https://www.overton.io), institutional login. Search your name or paper title. Results show every policy document that cites your work, including the issuing body, country, document type, and date. Indexes government documents, parliamentary papers, WHO/UN guidelines, think-tank outputs, and NGO policy briefs globally.
- **SAGE Policy Profiles (free):** [policyprofiles.sagepub.com](https://policyprofiles.sagepub.com). Search your name or paper title. Strong coverage of Australian state and federal health policy and international clinical and policy citations. More accurate than Overton for health and social science, though narrower in scope.
- **PlumX Metrics (via Scopus):** [scopus.com](https://www.scopus.com), your paper, PlumX Metrics panel, Social Media, Policy Citations. Coverage is less complete than Overton or SAGE but available without additional login.
- **Google Scholar:** Search your paper title in quotes and manually review results for government, WHO, or NGO documents. Labour-intensive but free, and captures documents not indexed elsewhere.

**Record as:** *'6 policy citations (Overton, June 2026)'* or *'4 policy citations (SAGE Policy Profiles, June 2026)'*. When citing policy documents in a grant, copy the full document title, issuing body, and year from Overton or SAGE for accuracy.

## 7. Altmetric Attention Score

**What to report:** The Altmetric Attention Score for your key publications. This is a weighted composite score that captures online attention across news outlets, blogs, social media, Wikipedia, policy documents, and post-publication peer review platforms. Many Australian grant schemes (NHMRC, ARC) now recognise Altmetric scores as evidence of broader engagement and societal impact.

**Where to find it:**

- **Altmetric Explorer:** [altmetric.com/explorer](https://www.altmetric.com/explorer), institutional access. Search by author name, paper title, or DOI. Each output displays its Altmetric doughnut and score. Export all scores to CSV for reporting.
- **Altmetric Bookmarklet (free):** [altmetric.com/products/free-tools/bookmarklet](https://www.altmetric.com/products/free-tools/bookmarklet). Install in your browser, then click it on any article page with a DOI to see the score instantly.
- **Dimensions (free tier):** [app.dimensions.ai](https://app.dimensions.ai). Search your paper, the Altmetric score appears alongside citations and other metrics.

**Record as:** *'Altmetric Attention Score of 87 for [Paper Title] (Altmetric, June 2026)'*. For grant applications, report scores for your top 3-5 outputs and note that scores above 20 place a paper in the top 5% of all research outputs tracked by Altmetric.

## 8. Downloads and Usage Metrics

**What to report:** Download counts and full-text views for your publications. These demonstrate reach beyond citation networks and are particularly useful for recent publications that have not yet accrued citations.

**Where to find it:**

- **PlumX Metrics (via Scopus):** [scopus.com](https://www.scopus.com), search your paper, PlumX Metrics panel, Usage tab. Shows abstract views, full-text views, and link-outs.
- **Publisher Dashboards:** Most publishers provide author dashboards with download statistics. Check your SAGE, Elsevier, Wiley, Taylor & Francis, or Springer Nature author portal.
- **Institutional Repository:** Your university's research repository (e.g., eSpace, ORCA, OPUS) tracks downloads for deposited outputs. Contact your library for access to these statistics.
- **SSRN / ResearchGate:** If you post preprints or accepted manuscripts, both platforms show download and read counts on your profile.

**Record as:** *'1,240 full-text downloads for [Paper Title] (Scopus PlumX, June 2026)'* or *'3,800 reads across ResearchGate profile (ResearchGate, June 2026)'*

---

## 9. Collaboration Metrics

**What to report:** Evidence of research collaboration, including international co-authorship, interdisciplinary partnerships, and industry engagement. Grant panels value collaborative capacity as a marker of research maturity and network strength.

**Where to find it:**

- **SciVal:** [scival.com](https://www.scival.com), institutional login. Researchers, search your name, Collaboration tab. Shows international, national, and institutional co-authorship percentages, plus a map of collaborating countries and institutions.
- **Scopus:** [scopus.com](https://www.scopus.com), your Author Profile, Co-authors tab. Lists all co-authors, their affiliations, and number of shared publications.
- **VOSviewer (free):** [vosviewer.com](https://www.vosviewer.com). Export your publication list from Scopus or Web of Science and import into VOSviewer to generate co-authorship network visualisations.

**Record as:** *'International co-authorship on 9 of 12 publications (75%), with collaborators across 6 countries (SciVal, June 2026)'*

---

## 10. Supervision and Training Impact

**What to report:** Higher degree research (HDR) supervision completions and current candidatures. This demonstrates research leadership, capacity building, and contribution to the next generation of researchers.

**Where to find it:**

- **Your institution's research office:** Request a formal supervision record showing completed and current HDR candidatures (PhD, Masters by Research, Professional Doctorates).
- **Your CV / university profile:** Most university staff profiles list current and completed supervisions.

**Record as:** *'Supervised 4 PhD completions (2 as principal advisor, 2 as associate advisor) and currently supervising 3 PhD candidates'*

---

## 11. Funding Track Record

**What to report:** Total competitive research funding awarded, broken down by role (CIA, CIB, etc.), funding body, and amount. Grant panels assess your ability to attract and manage funding.

**Where to find it:**

- **Your institution's research office:** Request a grants report showing all competitive funding with scheme name, funding body, your role, total award, and project dates.
- **ARC and NHMRC portals:** For Australian researchers, your Research Management System (RMS) profile lists all ARC and NHMRC applications and outcomes.
- **ORCID:** [orcid.org](https://orcid.org). If you have linked your funding records, they appear under the Funding section of your profile.

**Record as:** *'Total competitive research funding: $2.4M across 6 grants (3 as CIA) from NHMRC, ARC, and MRFF (2018-2026)'*

## Access to These Platforms

Most Australian universities provide access to these platforms through their library subscriptions. Log in using your university email address via the library portal. To access a platform: go to your university library website, search the platform name (for example, Scopus or SciVal), click the library link, and log in with your university credentials.

The following platforms are free and do not require institutional access:
- Google Scholar ([scholar.google.com](https://scholar.google.com))
- Scimago Journal Rank ([scimagojr.com](https://www.scimagojr.com))
- SAGE Policy Profiles ([policyprofiles.sagepub.com](https://policyprofiles.sagepub.com))
- Google Alerts ([google.com/alerts](https://www.google.com/alerts))
- Altmetric Bookmarklet ([altmetric.com/products/free-tools/bookmarklet](https://www.altmetric.com/products/free-tools/bookmarklet))
- Dimensions free tier ([app.dimensions.ai](https://app.dimensions.ai))
- VOSviewer ([vosviewer.com](https://www.vosviewer.com))
- ORCID ([orcid.org](https://orcid.org))

These are good starting points if you do not have a current university affiliation.

If you have any trouble accessing or extracting these metrics, please get in touch. We are happy to help.

## Tips for Presenting Metrics in Grant Applications

1. **Lead with your strongest metrics.** If your h-index is competitive, lead with it. If your policy citations are exceptional, foreground those. Tailor emphasis to the grant scheme's priorities.
2. **Use context and benchmarks.** Raw numbers mean little without context. Compare your metrics to field norms where possible (e.g., "h-index of 14, which places me in the top 20% of researchers at equivalent career stage in this field").
3. **Explain trajectory, not just totals.** A rising citation curve or accelerating publication rate signals momentum. Note your 5-year trend if it strengthens your case.
4. **Match metrics to impact claims.** If you claim policy impact, back it with Overton or SAGE policy citation data. If you claim public engagement, show Altmetric scores and media mentions.
5. **Be consistent across platforms.** Pick one platform per metric and use it throughout the application. Mixing Google Scholar citations with a Scopus h-index creates confusion.
6. **Date everything.** Metrics change daily. Always include the retrieval date so assessors can verify.
