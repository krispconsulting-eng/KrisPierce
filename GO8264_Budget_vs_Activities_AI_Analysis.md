#### Activity 2 - National DEE Resource Library ($742,592 / 29.2%)

**What's funded:**
- Implementation & Evaluation Lead (0.2 FTE, Academic Level B, from month 6)
- Senior Information & Advice Lead (0.6 FTE, Level 6, from month 6)
- Evidence synthesis, clinical review, content development
- Knowledge Management Platform (commercial) - licensing, implementation, ongoing support

**What the AWP commits to but the budget must stretch to cover:**
- "Core resource suite" covering diagnosis, family guides, education, NDIS, transition, wellbeing
- Annual evidence review and content updates for 5 years
- WCAG-compliant website/platform
- Version control and evidence review processes

**Gaps:**
- **No dedicated web/platform development budget beyond the commercial KM platform licence.** The AWP promises a WCAG-compliant digital platform, but the budget relies entirely on a commercial off-the-shelf product. If customisation is needed, there's no contingency.
- **Year 1 ramp is slow** (staff from month 6) but the AWP says "Develop Year 1." Only 6 months of staffing in Year 1 means compressed delivery.
- **Content volume vs staff.** Two part-time staff producing a comprehensive resource suite across multiple DEE conditions, plus pathway guides, plus education content, is ambitious.

**Assessment:** AMBER - Deliverable scope is wide for the staffing level. KM Platform is a single commercial dependency with no customisation budget.

#### Activity 3 - IAR Service ($39,000 / 1.5%)

**What's funded:**
- Enquiry management platform (software licensing, implementation, integration)
- Specialist accessibility supports and interpreter services
- Year 1 is $15k (implementation), then $6k/year (operating)

**What the AWP commits to:**
- National telephone, email, web-based enquiries and scheduled virtual appointments
- Individualised information and referral
- Service monitoring, enquiry analysis and continuous improvement
- National geographic reach including rural/remote
- Quarterly performance reports

**Gaps:**
- **No dedicated IAR staff are budgeted here.** The budget note says "Existing project staff deliver the IAR Service" (Coordinator + Lived Experience Lead + Senior Info Lead from Activity 1 & 2). This means IAR delivery competes with governance, content development, and everything else those staff do.
- **$6,000/year for a "national" service** is extremely lean. Interpreter services alone could exhaust this if demand materialises from CALD communities.
- **No after-hours provision.** The AWP doesn't promise it, but families of children with DEE frequently need information outside business hours. This is a service gap the AI case directly addresses.
- **Scalability risk.** If the service gets traction (which is the goal), there's no surge capacity. One complex enquiry can consume hours of a peer connector's time.

**Assessment:** RED - The AWP promises a national IAR service, but the budget funds only a software platform and minimal operating costs. Staff time is borrowed from other activities. This is the most stretched line in the budget.

#### Activity 4 - Accessible Resource Production ($47,000 / 1.8%)

**What's funded:**
- One-off allocation in Year 2 only: graphic design, WCAG review, Easy Read production, NAATI translation, First Nations cultural adaptation, digital publishing

**What the AWP commits to:**
- Easy Read resources
- Culturally adapted resources
- Accessible digital formats
- WCAG-compliant website
- "Initial release Year 1; expand Years 2-5"

**Gaps:**
- **$47,000 in a single year for all accessible formats, translations, and cultural adaptation is very tight.** Professional NAATI translation runs $0.20-0.30/word; a single 3,000-word resource in 5 languages = $3,000-4,500. Easy Read production is $2,000-5,000 per document. Graphic design and layout adds more.
- **Nothing budgeted in Years 3-5** despite the AWP promising expansion. Any new resources developed in Years 3-5 would need accessible versions but have no production budget.
- **First Nations cultural adaptation** is more than translation - it requires community engagement, co-design, and iterative review. $47k shared across all formats is insufficient for genuine cultural adaptation.

**Assessment:** RED - One-year funding for a five-year commitment. The AWP promises ongoing expansion of accessible formats but the budget is a single Year 2 allocation. This is a clear gap.

## Part 2: What's Missing in the Current Plan

### Structural Gaps (before AI is considered)

1. **No technology/digital infrastructure line.** The AWP promises a national digital platform, WCAG compliance, enquiry management, integrated systems - but technology is scattered across Activity 2 (KM platform) and Activity 3 (enquiry platform) with no architecture, integration, or contingency budget.

2. **Accessibility and translation is a one-shot.** $47k in Year 2, nothing thereafter. The AWP commits to "expand Years 2-5" but can't fund it.

3. **IAR service delivery is unfunded.** Staff time is borrowed. If the service works, it will cannibalise time from content development and governance.

4. **No contingency.** Five-year programs always encounter scope changes, policy shifts, cost increases. There is no contingency allocation.

5. **Content maintenance after Year 2 relies on goodwill.** The evidence review commitment is annual but the staffing profile doesn't grow, so maintenance competes with new development.

6. **Category B sustainability narrative is aspirational.** The AWP says "sustainability arrangements implemented before project completion" but the budget doesn't fund what makes it sustainable - only the plan.

## Part 4: What Adding AI Fixes in the Current Plan

### Gaps AI Directly Addresses

| Current Gap | AI Area That Fixes It |
|---|---|
| IAR service is unfunded (staff borrowed) | **Area 2** (Triage & Drafting) - AI handles volume, humans handle complexity |
| No after-hours service | **Area 1** (Library Assistant) - available 24/7, escalates to humans in business hours |
| Accessible production is one-shot ($47k Year 2 only) | **Area 3** (Resources in Every Format) - ongoing production at low marginal cost |
| Content maintenance unfunded after Year 2 | **Area 5** (Freshness Monitoring) - automates the detection, humans do the fixing |
| Evaluation data collection is manual | **Area 6** (Reporting & Intelligence) - continuous automated data |
| Category B sustainability is aspirational | **Areas 1, 3, 5 combined** - the "outcomes continue" story has a mechanism |
| Pathway guides are static PDFs | **Area 4** (Interactive Navigators) - personalised, locally relevant, from vetted content |

### What AI Does NOT Fix (Still Missing)

1. **Contingency allocation.** Still no buffer for scope changes, cost increases, or policy shifts.
2. **Evaluation timing.** The $62,402 in Year 3 still needs reconciling with Mid-Point (Year 4) and Final (Year 5) evaluation commitments. AI reporting data makes evaluation easier and cheaper, but the independent evaluator still needs funding.
3. **Conference/dissemination budget.** Activity 5 is still $2k/year. AI doesn't present at conferences.
4. **First Nations partnership depth.** The First Nations Lead (0.4 FTE) is funded, but genuine community partnership (travel, time, reciprocal engagement) is expensive and can't be automated. The current travel allocation embedded in Activity 1 may not be sufficient.
5. **Human review capacity for AI outputs.** AI Areas 2 and 3 produce drafts that humans review. The current staff need capacity to do this review on top of their other work. If AI generates volume, review becomes a bottleneck unless staff time is ring-fenced.
6. **AI governance and safety oversight.** The risk register has no AI-specific risks. If AI is added, the risk register needs entries for: model hallucination/misinformation, data privacy in AI processing, AI bias, accessibility of AI interfaces, vendor lock-in, and community trust/acceptance of AI in disability services.

## Part 5: Category B Sustainability - How AI Answers the Hard Question

The assessor asks: *"How does this survive 30 June 2032 without perpetual funding?"*

### The Four Mechanisms (with budget backing)

| Mechanism | What the AI Budget Funds | Post-Grant Cost |
|---|---|---|
| **Build once, run cheap** | $559k over 5 years builds the system; validated and operational by Year 3 | Hosting + API costs: ~$15-25k/year (Areas 1, 2, 4 combined) |
| **Self-maintaining content** | Area 5 built in Years 1-2, operational Years 2-5 | Flags what needs updating; small team maintains. ~$5k/year |
| **Owned and portable** | Consortium owns corpus, data, IP. Built on open standards. | No vendor lock-in. Can migrate. |
| **Degrades gracefully** | AI layer keeps core information function alive if human team contracts | Library Assistant + Pathway Navigators run without staff. IAR triage queues for reduced team. |

**Post-grant minimum viable cost: ~$20-30k/year** for hosting, API costs, and content maintenance - fundable through partner contributions, modest fees for professional users, or a fraction of one FTE equivalent.

## Part 6: Risk Register Additions Needed for AI

If AI is included, the following risks should be added:

| # | Risk | Likelihood | Consequence | Rating | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 15 | AI system produces inaccurate or misleading health/disability information | Possible | Major | High | Closed-corpus retrieval only; confidence thresholds; mandatory source citations; human review of edge cases; regular accuracy audits |
| 16 | Community resistance to AI in disability services | Possible | Moderate | Medium | Co-design with lived experience advisory; transparent about what AI does/doesn't do; always clear route to human; opt-out available |
| 17 | AI vendor dependency or cost escalation | Unlikely | Moderate | Medium | Portable architecture; no single-vendor lock-in; cost caps in contracts; open-source alternatives identified |
| 18 | Privacy/data protection in AI processing | Possible | Major | High | Privacy by design; no PII in model training; Australian data residency; UNSW privacy framework applies; audit logging |
| 19 | AI accessibility not meeting WCAG for users with disability | Possible | Moderate | Medium | Accessibility engineering from build; user testing with people with disability; screen reader compatibility; Easy Read interface option |

## Summary

**The current budget is well-structured for a human-delivered service** but has clear gaps in IAR service delivery capacity, accessible resource production beyond Year 2, and the sustainability mechanism that Category B demands.

**Adding the 6 AI areas at ~$559k (22% of current grant)** directly addresses the three weakest budget lines (Activities 3, 4, and 6), provides the concrete sustainability mechanism Category B requires, and transforms the service from staff-dependent to infrastructure-enabled.

**The AI-company partnership structure** (consortium owns IP, portable architecture, capability uplift) is the right model - it makes the AI investment a durable asset rather than an ongoing dependency.

**Recommended next step:** Decide whether to absorb the $559k within the existing $2.54M envelope (which would require reducing Activity 1 staffing or scope) or to increase the total grant ask to ~$3.1M. The grant opportunity guidelines maximum will determine which approach is feasible.
