# Mum

## Rule one

**Readers must miss her.** Which means the book must first make them love
her. Mum before Alzheimer's gets more page-time than Alzheimer's — the
disease only devastates a reader who knew what it took.

## Arc

Mother → Grandmother → Subtle change → Alzheimer's → Loss → **Legacy**

## Biography — Mum in her prime (the most important section)

- Where was she from, what shaped her, what was her own mother like?
- What did she do — work, home, community? What was she known for?
- Her voice: phrases she always said, how she answered the phone, what her
  laugh was like.
- Her hands: what do you see them doing when you picture her? (Cooking what?
  Making what? Holding whom?)

## Shaping questions

### Before
- What's the single scene that IS your mother — if a stranger could watch one memory, which one shows her whole?
- What did she teach you about mothering that you used with Will and Ella? What did you deliberately do differently?
- How did she respond when Will was diagnosed? What kind of grandmother was she to him — and to Ella?
- What did you two fight about, ever? (Love without friction reads as fiction.)
- What did she want for you that you did or didn't become?

### The subtle change
- What was the first thing you noticed — the tiny wrongness you explained away? (This scene, precisely rendered, is one every Alzheimer's family will recognise instantly.)
- Who said the word "Alzheimer's" first, and where?
- Did the nurse in you spot it early? Did you hide the knowing again, the way you did with Will's first signs? (If yes — that rhyme between the two diagnoses may be one of the book's most powerful structural echoes.)

### The long goodbye
- What went first, and what stayed longest? What could she still do near the end that surprised everyone?
- Was there a last fully-her day you didn't know was the last?
- Did she forget Will's diagnosis — and did that mean she loved him unshadowed? Did she forget you? Write the moment she first didn't know you, if it happened.
- How did you carer-for-Will and carer-for-Mum collide in practice — the same week, the same day, the same hour? Give one real logistical collision as a scene.
- What did Dad do, and what did it cost him?

### Loss and legacy
- Where were you when she died? What did you do that day and the day after?
- What of her lives on in you, in Ella, in the family's rituals? Name the specific tradition, recipe, phrase, gesture.
- What would she say about this book? About the charity? About who you became?
- What do you still talk to her about?
