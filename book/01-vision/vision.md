# Vision — The Families We Become

## Working title

**The Families We Become**

The plural matters: this family was made and remade more than once — by IVF,
by diagnosis, by siblinghood, by Alzheimer's, by grief, by advocacy. The
title also holds the reader's own story: every family becomes something it
didn't plan to be.

*(Subtitle candidates — collect, judge later:)*
- *A memoir of love, care and the lives we didn't plan*
- *What caring for the people we love makes of us*
- —

## Vision

To create a deeply moving, commercially successful memoir that explores how
one family was reshaped by IVF, rare disease, siblinghood, Alzheimer's,
grief and advocacy, while speaking to anyone whose life has unfolded
differently from what they expected.

## Purpose

Create a book that:

- Resonates far beyond the rare disease community
- Honours the family
- Changes how people think about caregiving
- Leaves a lasting legacy for Will, Ella and future generations
- Has the quality and positioning to become a bestselling memoir

## Narrative promise — the thesis (v2, from the motif work)

> **This is a book about how one family kept reinventing itself every time
> life asked it to become something new.** Each reinvention wasn't chosen —
> it was called for by what life placed in front of the family. Readers who
> have never heard of SCN2A finish it understanding something new about
> their own lives, because every family is eventually asked to become
> something it didn't plan.

The reinvention chain (the whole cast, not just the narrator):
- Kris: mother after IVF → rare disease parent → advocate → national leader → founder → entrepreneur and author
- Ella: a sibling in extraordinary circumstances → an individual with her own voice
- David: a father navigating uncertainty → the other half of "our work"
- Mum: grandmother → a woman living with Alzheimer's
- Bob: a husband caring for his wife → a father supporting his daughter

The emotional spine is **care across generations** — three caregiving
stories, not one: becoming a mother through IVF; caring for a child with a
rare disease; caring for Mum through Alzheimer's.

Earlier framings (kept as pull-quote candidates):
- *A family is not what happens to you — it is what you make, again and again, out of what happens to you.*
- *You don't get the family you planned. You become the family your life asks of you.*
- *Care is not what shrank this life. Care is what built it.*

## Reader profile (DRAFT — for Kris to react to)

**Primary reader:** an adult — most often a woman, 30–70 — who has lived a
family reshaping of her own: a caregiver, a parent whose child's life
diverged from the imagined one, an adult child losing a parent to dementia,
a sibling who grew up alongside needs bigger than her own. She reads memoir
for company, not information.

**Secondary readers:**
- Health professionals, researchers and policy people who want to understand
  the family side of the systems they run
- The rare disease and dementia communities
- Readers of literary memoir generally — she picks the book up because the
  writing is good, not because of the subject

**She is NOT:** a reader looking for a medical explainer, an advocacy
pamphlet, or an inspirational tract. (Guard rail: story before advocacy.)

## Core themes

Hope · Motherhood · Care · Loss · Identity · Resilience · Leadership ·
Grief · Love · Systems · Purpose · Belonging · **Women's health**

## Success criteria

The project succeeds if readers say:

> **"I saw my own family in yours."**

Not: "I learned about SCN2A." Not: "I learned about Alzheimer's."
The diseases are the context. The family is the story.

Secondary success measures (publishing stream):
- [ ] Manuscript reaches V2 (developmental + structural edit complete)
- [ ] Represented by an agent / acquired by a publisher (or a deliberate,
      well-executed alternative path)
- [ ] Platform live: website, speaking strategy, media plan
- [ ] Family review completed and family proud of the result

## Ethical boundaries (OPEN — needs Kris; decide before drafting)

These decisions unlock free drafting. Undecided boundaries create fearful
writing.

1. **Will.** How is Will's consent/assent handled, given his communication
   and capacity? Who speaks for his dignity on the page? Principle proposed:
   *nothing on the page Will could one day encounter and feel diminished by;
   whole person, never diagnosis.*
2. **Ella.** She is old enough to have a view. Does she get a preview? A
   veto over her own scenes? (Proposed: yes to both.)
3. **Husband.** Review rights over the marriage material? What's agreed
   off-limits?
4. **Mum.** She cannot consent. What would she have wanted on the page?
   Who in the family should read her chapters first? (Principle: readers
   must miss her — dignity is the vehicle, not the obstacle.)
5. **Dad and extended family.** Preview? Named?
6. **Professionals** (doctors, researchers): real names, changed names, or
   role-only ("the neurologist")?
7. **The hard truths.** Which uncomfortable emotional truths are IN
   (truth over perfection) and which specifics are OUT (family first)?
   List both explicitly.
8. **Medical detail.** How much of Will's medical record belongs on the
   page? Proposed test: only what the scene needs.
