# Themes

Twelve threads. A theme earns its place by recurring in scenes, not by
being announced. For each: what the book argues about it, and the questions
that find its scenes.

## Hope
The book's opening note (the emotional arc starts here). What the book might argue: hope is not optimism — it's a discipline that changes its object: baby → cure → good days → meaning.
- What did you hope for at each stage, and how did the hope itself change shape?
- Was hope ever the enemy — the thing that made a setback unbearable?

## Motherhood
- What kind of mother did you plan to be? What kind did each child actually need?
- Where did motherhood and nursing blur — and did the blur protect you or rob you?

## Care
The book's central subject: care as work, identity, economy, love.
- What does a day of care physically consist of? (Readers must feel the labour.)
- Who cares for the carer? What happened when the answer was nobody?
- What did being cared FOR feel like, on the rare occasions it happened?

## Loss
- Rank the losses honestly: which cost most — the imagined child, the imagined life, Mum, friendships, career, self?
- What did you lose that nobody sent a card for? (Ambiguous loss — the losses without funerals — may be the book's sharpest insight.)

## Identity
- Finish honestly: "Without anyone to care for, I would be ___."
- Which identity in the arc (nurse, mother, carer, advocate, founder) fit best? Which was a costume?

## Resilience
- What does the word "resilient" get wrong about you? (The book can push back on the compliment that traps carers.)
- What did surviving actually require — the unglamorous mechanics: lists, casseroles, medication charts, dark jokes?

## Leadership
- When did you first realise you were leading — and who followed before you noticed?
- What does leading from lived experience allow that professional leadership can't? What does it cost that professionals never pay?

## Grief
- How many kinds of grief does this story hold? (Diagnosis grief, anticipatory grief, Alzheimer's "long goodbye," bereavement, community grief.) Name each and its scene.
- Which grief are you still in?

## Love
- Where does love live in this book when it can't live in words — Will's love, late-stage Mum's love? The book's job is showing love that has no speech.
- What's the most loved you've ever felt?

## Systems
- The thesis question: do our systems assume a family like yours exists to absorb what they don't provide? Say what the book argues.
- One scene where a system worked. One where it failed. One where you beat it. One where it changed because of you.

## Purpose
- When did pain become purpose — and was that transformation a gift, a tax, or both?
- What would you have chosen if purpose hadn't chosen you?

## Belonging
- Where do you belong most completely? (The answer might be surprising — a ward, a conference of strangers who understand, the kitchen at midnight.)
- What communities did the family lose, and which unexpected ones did it find?

## Cross-cutting questions
- Which TWO themes are the real spine? (Twelve threads, but a book argues one or two things. Candidates: **care** and **identity/becoming** — the title suggests both.)
- Which theme scares you most to write? That's usually the one the book is actually about.
