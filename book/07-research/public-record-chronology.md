## Identity anchor (all 3-0 verified, high confidence)

Kris (published as Kristine) Pierce — Australian nurse and rare-disease
advocate; founder of SCN2A Australia (co-founded with husband **Dr David
Cunnington**, c.2017 — the year is self-reported in bios); co-founder of
Genetic Epilepsy Team Australia (GETA); mother of **Will**, who had
seizures from **day 4 of life**, was **misdiagnosed with Alpers Syndrome in
his first year and sent home for palliative care with advice he would live
only a few more months**, and received his SCN2A diagnosis **at age 14**
via Prof Ingrid Scheffer's genetic epilepsy research panel.

## What this changes for the book (memoir implications)

1. **The diagnostic odyssey is 14 years long.** The architecture assumed a
   single early "diagnosis day" pivot. The verified shape is harsher and
   structurally richer: seizures at day 4 → Alpers misdiagnosis → *sent home
   to watch your baby die* → he lives → **13 more years without a name** →
   SCN2A at 14. That's not one before/after line; it's two — the day life
   changed, and the day it finally had a name. Part II needs rebuilding
   around this.
2. **The palliative-care scene** — being told your infant has months, and
   then those months not ending — may be the most powerful chapter in the
   book, and almost no reader will have seen it written from inside.
3. **The twins question** (above) — resolve before Ella's thread is drafted.
4. **The public record is thin on the private story** — no mainstream news
   coverage found, no parliamentary submissions verified. Good news for the
   book: the story is largely untold. The memoir isn't competing with its
   own press coverage.
