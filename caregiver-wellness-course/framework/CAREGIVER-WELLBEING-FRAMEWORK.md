# Caregiver Wellbeing Framework

A trusted companion for parents and family caregivers of children with lifelong disability, complex medical needs and rare diseases.

---

## About this document

This is the master framework for the Caregiver Wellbeing Framework. It sets out what we are making, who it is for, how it is designed, and the standards every resource must meet. It is written first for Australian families, and built so that charities, hospitals, health services, researchers and government-funded programmes can adopt it, adapt it and trust it.

Everything that follows exists to serve one quiet promise: that a parent who is stretched thin will find something here that helps, and will not feel judged, sold to, or asked to become someone they are not.

---

## 1. Vision

Most wellbeing content asks caregivers to do more. It arrives as another login, another module, another list of things they are apparently failing to do. For a parent who is already carrying an enormous, mostly invisible load, that is not support. It is one more weight.

This framework is built to be the opposite of that.

We want caregivers to experience this as a trusted companion, not another online course. Something that sits alongside them, meets them where they are, and understands the life they are actually living. It should feel like being met by someone who gets it, who is not going to tell them to try harder, and who has something genuinely useful to offer in the small amount of time and energy they can spare.

A companion does not demand. It does not grade. It does not disappear when things get hard. It is steady, it is honest, and it is on their side.

If a caregiver comes away from any part of this framework feeling calmer, a little more understood, and holding one small thing they can actually do, we have succeeded.

---

## 2. Guiding philosophy

This is important, so we will say it plainly.

**This framework is not about becoming a better caregiver.**

The parents and family members we are serving are already devoted, already skilled, already doing far more than most people will ever understand. They do not need to be improved. The premise that they are falling short is exactly the premise we reject.

Instead, this framework helps caregivers:

- **Understand what is happening to their wellbeing.** Caring for a child with lifelong disability, complex medical needs or a rare disease changes a person. Naming what is happening, and why, is the first relief.
- **Normalise common experiences.** So much of what caregivers feel, they feel in silence, assuming they are the only one. They are not. Seeing their experience reflected back reduces the loneliness of it.
- **Reduce guilt.** Guilt is one of the heaviest and most constant companions of caregiving. We work to loosen its grip, not add to it.
- **Increase confidence and self-awareness.** Not confidence as performance, but the quiet confidence that comes from understanding yourself and trusting your own read on your situation.
- **Encourage realistic, sustainable changes.** Small changes that fit inside a real life, that survive a hard week, and that do not collapse the moment something goes wrong.
- **Recognise their own strengths.** Caregivers routinely underestimate their own resourcefulness, resilience and love. We help them see it.
- **Offer hope, without unrealistic expectations.** Honest hope. Not a promise that things will get easier or that a good attitude fixes a hard reality, but a genuine, grounded sense that their wellbeing matters and can be tended.

We are not trying to fix caregivers. We are trying to help them feel understood, and to give them something small and real to hold on to.

## 3. The educational model

### The blend

Every resource is built on a deliberate balance:

- **30% evidence.** Enough to explain what is happening and why it makes sense, grounded in credible sources, always in plain language.
- **50% guided reflection.** The heart of the work. We help caregivers turn the evidence inward, gently, so it becomes personal and useful rather than abstract.
- **20% practical action.** One small, doable thing. Never a to-do list. Never homework.

This ratio matters. If we lead with evidence, we lecture. If we lead with action, we add pressure. Reflection is where understanding turns into relief, and relief is where change becomes possible.

### The four feelings

Every single resource, no matter how small, is designed to leave a caregiver with four feelings:

1. **"I understand myself a little better."**
2. **"I'm not alone."**
3. **"What I'm experiencing makes sense."**
4. **"I have one small thing I can do."**

If a resource does not produce these four feelings, it is not finished. This is our test. Before anything is published, we ask: will an exhausted parent, reading this at the end of a long day, come away feeling these four things? If not, we rework it.

## 4. Tone of voice

The tone is the framework. If we get the tone wrong, none of the content will land, however good it is.

### How we sound

Warm. Calm. Encouraging. Validating. Respectful. Practical. Evidence informed. Quietly hopeful.

We write the way a wise, kind friend would speak: someone who has sat with hard things, who does not flinch, who does not perform positivity, and who tells the truth gently.

### What we never do

- **No toxic positivity.** We never tell a caregiver to look on the bright side, be grateful, or that everything happens for a reason.
- **No motivational clichés.** No "you've got this", no "be your best self", no slogans.
- **No productivity language.** We are not optimising anyone. No "hacks", no "routines to maximise", no "level up".
- **No perfectionism.** Nothing here is to be done perfectly, or fully, or every day.
- **No guilt or judgement.** We never imply a caregiver is doing too little, or doing it wrong.
- **No fear-based messaging.** We do not frighten people into action with warnings about what will happen if they do not look after themselves.
- **No clinical or academic language.** Evidence is translated. Jargon is removed.
- **No AI clichés and no generic wellness language.** No "dive in", no "unlock", no "journey" as a filler, no "holistic", no "nurture your soul". We write like a person, for a person.

### The phrase we never use

We never say **"prioritise self-care."** It is hollow, it is everywhere, and to a caregiver with no spare hours it can feel like a small cruelty. We talk instead about small, specific, real things, and we let the caregiver decide what fits.

### Mechanics

- **Australian English** throughout. Recognise, organise, colour, programme (for a structured course), centre, and so on.
- **No em dashes.** We use commas, colons and semicolons to carry the rhythm. Where a pause or aside is needed, a comma or a colon does the work.
- Short sentences are welcome. White space is welcome. We do not crowd the page.

### Words to avoid

We avoid "just" and "simply" (they minimise real difficulty), and we avoid "you should" (it instructs rather than invites). We prefer "you might", "some caregivers find", "when you are ready", "if it helps".

## 5. Audience assumptions

We assume the caregiver reading this is:

- **Exhausted.** Often profoundly, and often for years, not days.
- **Short on time.** Minutes, not hours. Sometimes interrupted mid-sentence.
- **Isolated.** Many have lost friendships, careers and community to the demands of care.
- **Possibly grieving.** Grief for the child's diagnosis, for the life they imagined, for their own former self. Grief that has no clear end.
- **Possibly uncertain or burnt out.** Living with unpredictability, medical instability, or a slow erosion of their own reserves.
- **An expert in their child's condition.** Often more knowledgeable than many of the clinicians they meet. We never talk down to them about their child.
- **Managing competing demands.** Other children, work, relationships, their own health, finances, and the relentless administration of care.

Because of this, we:

- **Value practical support** over inspiration.
- **Respect their intelligence.** We do not oversimplify, patronise or pad. These are capable, thoughtful adults dealing with complexity every day.
- **Do not oversimplify the difficulty of their situation.** We name it honestly. Pretending it is easier than it is would break trust immediately.

## 6. Evidence standards

We are evidence informed, and we are honest about evidence.

### Australian evidence first

Because our first audience is Australian families, we prioritise Australian sources and Australian context:

- **Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS)**, including the Survey of Disability, Ageing and Carers.
- **Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW).**
- **Carers Australia** and the state and territory Carers organisations.
- **The Carer Wellbeing Survey** (Carers Australia and partners).
- **Carer Gateway** and its programmes and services.

### Wider evidence base

Alongside Australian sources, we draw on:

- Peer-reviewed **systematic reviews** and high-quality primary studies.
- **Implementation science**, so that what we recommend can actually be adopted and sustained.
- Literature on **caregiver wellbeing**, **rare disease**, **paediatric disability** and **behaviour change**.

### How we use evidence

- We **explain everything in plain language.** If a caregiver needs a glossary, we have failed to translate.
- **International evidence supports, it does not replace, Australian context.** We use global research to understand mechanisms and what helps, and we anchor the specifics, services and statistics in Australian reality.
- We are **honest about uncertainty.** Where evidence is mixed or thin, we say so.

### Integrity of figures

We name the source body and describe findings qualitatively in plain language. We do not invent precise statistics, percentages, sample sizes or citations. Where a specific current figure would strengthen a resource, we insert a clearly marked verification placeholder, for example `[VERIFY: latest figure from AIHW ___]`, so a human can confirm it against the current release before publication. Being qualitatively accurate with a flag is always better than stating a precise but unverified number.

## 7. Learning design principles

- **Guided conversation, not a lecture.** The reader should feel spoken with, not spoken at.
- **Storytelling.** Real-feeling caregiver stories (composite, never a real identifiable person) carry more truth than any statistic.
- **Scenarios.** We show recognisable moments from caregiving life, so the reader sees themselves.
- **Reflection.** We ask gentle, open questions and leave room for the reader to answer inwardly.
- **Curiosity.** We invite noticing and wondering, never testing or assessing.
- **Examples.** Concrete, specific and grounded in the everyday texture of caring for a child with complex needs.

---

## 8. The 7-part session structure

Every session, module and week follows the same seven parts. The consistency is deliberate: a familiar shape is restful, and a tired reader always knows where they are.

1. **Welcome.** A warm, calm opening. Set the tone, name the theme, lower the pressure. Make it safe to be here for two minutes or twenty.
2. **Caregiver story.** A composite story that reflects the theme. This is where the reader feels "I'm not alone."
3. **Evidence-informed learning.** The 30%. What is happening and why it makes sense, in plain language, grounded in credible sources.
4. **Guided reflection.** The 50%. Three or four gentle prompts that turn the learning inward.
5. **One small step.** The 20%. A single Tiny Step, realistically doable in under five minutes.
6. **Wellbeing Journey check-in.** A brief check against the eight dimensions of the Caregiver Wellbeing Wheel, so the caregiver can notice where they are without judgement.
7. **Looking ahead.** A soft close and a gentle bridge to what comes next, so the reader leaves with continuity, not a cliffhanger or a task.

## 9. Behaviour change principles

Our approach to change is defined in full in `TINY-STEPS-METHODOLOGY.md`. In brief, we hold to these principles:

- **Small, sustainable actions.** Always.
- **Never major lifestyle change.** We never ask a caregiver to overhaul their life, diet, sleep or routine. That advice ignores their reality and sets them up to feel like they failed.
- **Encourage noticing.** Awareness comes before change, and is valuable on its own.
- **Build confidence.** Each small success is evidence to the caregiver that they can tend their own wellbeing.
- **Reduce decision fatigue.** We make the next step obvious and small, so it costs almost no mental energy to begin.
- **Reinforce self-compassion.** We actively counter the harsh inner voice so many caregivers carry.
- **Celebrate small wins.** Genuinely, without gushing.
- **Normalise setbacks.** A missed week is not a failure. It is a week that was hard. Every week is a fresh start.
- **Every action realistically under five minutes.** If it takes longer, it is too big.

## 10. Resource development principles

Every printable or shareable resource is:

- **Printable.** It works on paper, because not everyone wants another screen, and paper survives a broken phone and a flat battery.
- **Visually simple and uncluttered.** Generous space. Nothing busy.
- **Calming** to look at.
- **Reusable.** It can be returned to again and again, not used up once.
- **Practical.** It earns its place.
- **Not homework.** Nothing is graded, collected or checked. There is no obligation.
- **Focused on one problem each.** One resource, one idea. We never crowd several things onto one sheet.

## 11. Australian context

We ground everything in Australian reality:

- **Australian terminology** for conditions, services and roles.
- **Australian health systems and pathways**, including public and private care, paediatric services and hospital in the home.
- **Australian supports and services**, named accurately so caregivers can actually find them.
- **The NDIS** (National Disability Insurance Scheme), including the realities of plans, reviews and navigating the scheme.
- **Carer Gateway**, its counselling, peer support, coaching and respite pathways.
- **Helplines and crisis supports** relevant to Australian families.

Where the framework is adapted for other countries, this section is localised while the philosophy, tone and educational model stay the same.

### A note on safety

Wellbeing content is not a substitute for medical or mental health care. Every resource includes a calm, non-alarming reminder of where to turn for urgent help, including in Australia: **000** for emergencies, **Lifeline on 13 11 14**, and **Carer Gateway on 1800 422 737**. These are offered as steady information, never as a warning.

## 12. Long-term vision: the full framework

The complete Caregiver Wellbeing Framework is made of the following components. Some exist now; others will be built over time. Together they form a coherent standard that an organisation can adopt in full or in part.

- **The Caregiver Wellbeing Wheel.** The eight-dimension model at the centre of the framework: Social, Occupational, Environmental, Intellectual, Spiritual, Emotional, Physical and Financial wellbeing, adapted specifically for caregivers of children with complex needs.
- **The wellbeing assessment.** A gentle, non-clinical self-check across the eight dimensions, used for the check-in and to track change over time without pressure.
- **The eight-week guided programme.** The core structured experience, one theme per week, each in the 7-part session structure.
- **The companion workbook.** A printable, reusable workbook that holds reflections and Tiny Steps.
- **The caregiver journal.** A lightweight, unstructured space for noticing, with no obligation to fill it.
- **The Tiny Steps methodology.** Our behaviour change approach, defined in `TINY-STEPS-METHODOLOGY.md`.
- **The resource library.** Standalone printable resources, each on one problem.
- **Facilitator materials.** Guides for peer facilitators, coaches and group leaders to deliver the programme with fidelity to the tone and philosophy.
- **Digital tools.** Optional gentle digital versions of the wheel, check-ins and prompts, built to reduce rather than add pressure.
- **Implementation resources.** Practical guidance for organisations adopting the framework, grounded in implementation science.
- **Hospital and charity versions.** Adaptations tuned for a hospital setting (including inpatient and clinic contexts) and for charity delivery.
- **Research validation.** A plan and instruments for evaluating outcomes, so the framework can earn and hold the trust of researchers and funders.
- **Continuing learning library.** An ongoing, growing collection for caregivers who want to keep going after the eight weeks, at their own pace, with no pressure to continue.

## The standard, in one line

Warm, honest, evidence informed, and small enough to fit a hard day: something that helps a caregiver feel understood, and gives them one real thing to hold on to.
