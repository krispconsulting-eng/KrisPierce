# Week 6: When it slips, and how to steady it

**Theme:** Taking honest stock of what has stuck and what has slipped, the mid-programme dip, and protecting the small things you have built.

*As with every week, there is nothing to complete and nothing to get right. Take what helps and leave the rest.*

---

## 1. Welcome

Welcome back. This week we are not opening any new ground. Instead, we are doing something that almost no course does honestly: stopping to look at what has actually happened over the past five weeks, including the parts that did not go to plan.

Here is our starting position, stated plainly. If some of your small steps have slipped, if you skipped a week, if the corner you cleared has silted back up with paperwork, nothing has gone wrong. That is not the programme failing, and it is certainly not you failing. It is what real life does to new practices, especially a life as unpredictable as yours.

Most wellbeing programmes quietly assume things go up and to the right. Yours may have gone sideways, backwards, or off the map entirely for a fortnight. This week is about looking at all of that without flinching, keeping what worked, and letting go of the rest without a backwards glance.

Two minutes still counts. Begin wherever you are.

---

## 2. Caregiver story

*This is a composite story. It is not a real, identifiable person, but it is built from experiences that many caregivers will recognise.*

Nick is dad to Isla, who is five and lives with a rare epilepsy syndrome. He came into a programme like this one sceptical, and then surprised himself. By week four he had a few small things going: three slow breaths while the coffee machine ran, a chair on the back verandah he had cleared of clutter and actually sat in some evenings, and a Sunday text to his brother.

Then Isla had a cluster of seizures and was admitted to hospital for nine days.

The breaths stopped. The verandah chair sat empty. His brother got no text. Nick slept in a fold-out beside Isla's bed, lived on vending machine coffee, and did what caregivers do: everything, for someone else, indefinitely. When they finally got home, he thought about the programme and felt a familiar, weary verdict forming: well, that's that ruined. He nearly did not come back to it. In his experience, things you fall off, you have failed. Diets, gym memberships, resolutions. Why would this be different?

Here is what Nick could not see from inside that week: nothing was ruined. The breaths were not a streak to break. The chair had not gone anywhere. His brother was not counting Sundays. Every one of his small things was sitting exactly where he left it, waiting, requiring no apology and no catching up. The only thing telling him he had failed was the voice that talks to every caregiver that way, and it was wrong.

He sat in the chair that night for four minutes. That was the restart. There was no ceremony to it.

## 3. Evidence-informed learning

Here is what is worth knowing this week.

**Sustaining a practice is harder than starting one, for everyone.** Behaviour change research is clear that the difficult part is not beginning something new; it is keeping it alive once novelty fades and life interferes. Motivation naturally dips in the middle of any structured programme, and researchers see this pattern so reliably that it has nothing to do with the character of the person in the middle of it. If weeks four and five felt flatter than weeks one and two, you are precisely on schedule.

**A brief, honest review helps more than pushing on.** Evidence from behaviour change research suggests that pausing to take stock, noticing what is working and what has slipped, and adjusting, improves follow-through compared with grimly pressing ahead as if nothing happened. The review has to be honest and it has to be kind; an audit conducted by your harshest inner voice does not count and does not help.

**For caregivers, the interruptions are structural, not personal.** Your practices do not slip because you are undisciplined. They slip because a seizure, an admission, a bad night or a broken piece of equipment can requisition your entire week without notice. This is why every step in this programme was built to survive disappearing for a while: no streaks, no catching up, nothing to rebuild. A practice you can return to without penalty is worth ten practices that punish absence.

**And the things you have built erode quietly if nobody guards them.** The cleared corner refills. The protected hour gets lent out. Research on carer respite points out that breaks and boundaries are easiest to surrender precisely because they are yours alone, with no one else to insist on them. Once something has proven it helps you, it deserves the same defence you would mount for any of your child's supports. You are allowed to be non-negotiable about something that is only for you.

## 4. Guided reflection

An honest, kind stocktake. No marks are being awarded, in either direction.

1. **Looking back over five weeks, what actually stuck, even partly?** A breath at the kettle, a named feeling, one text. However small, what came with you?

2. **What slipped, and what was happening when it slipped?** Notice, like Nick's hospital week, whether the slipping coincided with life getting harder. What does that tell you about whose fault it was?

3. **What does your inner voice say about the slipped things?** Is it delivering verdicts or information? What would the kinder, equally honest version of the same sentence sound like?

4. **Of everything you have tried since week 1, which one thing is most worth defending?** If you could keep only one, which would it be, and what currently threatens it?

## 5. One small step

Here is your Tiny Step for this week. Under a minute, nothing needed, and it doubles as your review.

**While the kettle boils, name one thing from these past weeks you want to keep.**

Next time you are waiting for the kettle, the coffee machine or the microwave, ask yourself one question: of the small things I have tried lately, which one do I want to keep? Name it silently. That is the whole step. You are not recommitting to everything, and you are not drawing up a plan. You are choosing one keeper.

If the honest answer is "none of them yet", that is a real answer, and naming it still counts as the step done in full.

## 6. Wellbeing Journey check-in

A brief check-in with the eight areas of the Caregiver Wellbeing Wheel. Noticing, never scoring.

This week, you might pay particular attention to two areas:

- **Occupational:** how is your sense of steadiness in the role itself, and in whatever work sits alongside it? Is anything you built in recent weeks helping you carry it, even slightly?
- **Environmental:** how are your spaces holding up? If you claimed a corner or cleared a surface in week 2, how is it faring, and does it need quietly reclaiming?

Glance at the other six if you have room: Social, Intellectual, Spiritual, Emotional, Physical, Financial. Six weeks in, an honest picture usually has some areas softer, some unchanged, and maybe one worse, because life kept happening while you did this. That mixed picture is not a disappointing result. It is a true one, and true is what we are after.
