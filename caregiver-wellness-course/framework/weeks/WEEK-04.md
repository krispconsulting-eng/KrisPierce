# Week 4: Money, work and the life you had planned

**Theme:** The real financial cost of caring, navigating support without drowning in it, and what caring does to work and working identity.

*As with every week, there is nothing to complete and nothing to get right. Take what helps and leave the rest.*

## 1. Welcome

Welcome back, and a quiet milestone worth naming: this is Week 4, the halfway point. However much or little you feel you have "done" so far, being here at week 4 is not nothing. You have kept a door open for yourself for a month, in a life with very few spare doors.

This week is practical ground: money and work. These two are talked about even less than grief, partly because they feel unseemly to raise. Loving a child and counting the cost of their care sit uncomfortably in the same sentence. So most caregivers do the counting silently, at midnight, over a spreadsheet or a bank app, and tell no one.

We are going to talk about it plainly. Not to solve your finances in a week, which no honest course would promise, but to name what caring really costs, in dollars and in careers, and to make the load a little easier to look at.

If money is a raw subject in your house right now, take this week especially gently.

## 2. Caregiver story

*This is a composite story. It is not a real, identifiable person, but it is built from experiences that many caregivers will recognise.*

Kate was a project manager. She was good at it: budgets, timelines, wrangling complicated things into order. When her son Max was diagnosed with a rare genetic condition at eighteen months, she took leave, then extended it, then went part-time, then, after the third hospital admission in a year made even part-time impossible, she resigned. Nobody made her. Every step was her choice, and every step was also not really a choice at all.

These days Kate runs the most complicated project of her life for no pay. She manages Max's NDIS plan like a contract, tracks therapy invoices, chases receipts, and knows the price of things she never expected to know the price of. The costs are not one big number. They are a thousand small ones: parking at the children's hospital, the power bill with machines running all night, the special formula, the travel, the equipment the funding did not quite cover, the income that stopped.

What catches her off guard is not the spreadsheet. It is the moments in between. A former colleague messaged recently: the team she built won an award. She was glad for them, and then she sat in the car and felt something she could not name for a while. It was not regret exactly. It was the version of herself that had an office and a title and a salary, waving from a road not taken.

When forms ask for her occupation, she still hesitates.

## 3. Evidence-informed learning

Here is what is worth knowing this week.

**The financial strain is real and widely shared.** Australian research on unpaid carers consistently finds that financial stress is one of the most common pressures they report, and that caring for a child with disability or complex medical needs brings both extra costs and reduced income at the same time [VERIFY: current Carer Wellbeing Survey and Carers Australia findings on carer financial stress and income impact]. If money feels tighter than it should for how hard you work, that is not mismanagement. It is the standard arithmetic of caring, and it is one of the least acknowledged parts of the whole role.

**Careers are commonly reshaped by caring.** Reduced hours, turned-down promotions, changed fields, paused studies, jobs left altogether: research on carers' employment shows these adjustments are extremely common, especially for mothers of children with high support needs [VERIFY: findings on carer workforce participation, ABS Survey of Disability, Ageing and Carers or AIHW]. This matters beyond the money. For many people, work carried identity, adult company, competence and a sense of a future. Losing or shrinking it is a genuine loss, and it deserves to be grieved as one, not brushed off as a logistics change.

**Support exists, and asking for it is not failure.** In Australia there is a patchwork of real support: Carer Payment and Carer Allowance through Services Australia, the NDIS for eligible children, and Carer Gateway (1800 422 737), which can also point to financial and planning support. The patchwork is genuinely tedious to navigate, and this week does not ask you to conquer it. It asks you to hold one idea: seeking every dollar and every support you are entitled to is not scrounging. It is part of doing this job properly, the same as it would be in any workplace.

One more thing the research suggests: caregivers report that even getting a clearer picture of their finances, without fixing anything yet, reduces the background dread. Known numbers are lighter than unknown ones.

## 4. Guided reflection

Money and identity are tender ground. Take only what feels useful.

1. **What has caring actually cost you?** Not to dwell, but to name it honestly, perhaps for the first time: the dollars, the income, the career steps, the plans. What would it be like to say the full list out loud, even once, even only to yourself?

2. **Like Kate in the car, is there a version of you waving from a road not taken?** What did work, or study, or that path give you besides money? Company, competence, being known for something?

3. **Is there support you have not pursued because it felt like too much admin, or too much like asking?** What is the one you suspect might actually be worth the paperwork?

4. **If a colleague did the job you do, unpaid, for years, what would you say their work was worth?** Notice how differently you cost your own labour.

## 5. One small step

Here is your Tiny Step for this week. Under a minute, nothing needed, works on a hard day.

**When the next bill or official letter arrives, write the single next action on it, then put it down.**

Do not solve it. On the envelope or a sticky note, write the one next thing: "ring this number", "ask about this charge", "file with NDIS papers". Then set it down. You have made it smaller, and you have taken back the weight of holding it in your head.

One letter, once this week, is the step done in full. The rest of the pile can stay a pile.

## 6. Wellbeing Journey check-in

A brief check-in with the eight areas of the Caregiver Wellbeing Wheel. Nothing is scored and nothing is judged.

This week, you might pay particular attention to two areas:

- **Financial:** how much background stress is money carrying at the moment? Is it a known load or an unknown one? Steady, stretched, or somewhere in between?
- **Occupational:** how is your sense of purpose and work, in the widest sense? That includes caring itself, paid work if you have it, and the working identity you carry. Is there anything in this area that feels like yours?

Glance kindly at the other six if you have room: Social, Environmental, Intellectual, Spiritual, Emotional, Physical. Halfway through the programme, some people notice small shifts; many notice none yet. Both are normal, and neither is a verdict.
