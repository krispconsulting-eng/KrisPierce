# Week 8: Looking back, and gently ahead

**Theme:** Your second Wellbeing Journey check-in, an honest look at what has shifted, and choosing what to carry forward.

*As with every week, there is nothing to complete and nothing to get right. Take what helps and leave the rest.*

---

## 1. Welcome

Welcome to Week 8, the last week of the programme.

Before anything else, take this in: you are a person with almost no spare time or energy, and you gave some of both to yourself, for eight weeks, in whatever amounts you could manage. Some weeks you may have read every word; others you may have skimmed a paragraph at midnight or missed entirely. All of it counts. There was never a version of this you could do wrong.

This final week has a simple shape. We look back at where you started, take the same gentle check-in you took in week 1 and set the two side by side, and then think about what comes next. Not a grand finale, and no certificate demanding you feel transformed. Just an honest look, and a considered choice about what goes with you.

One more thing, said plainly because it matters: finishing this course does not mean you should now have your wellbeing sorted. Your life is still your life. The load you carry is still real. The point was never to fix you, because you were never broken. The point was to give you back a little room, and a few small things that are yours.

---

## 2. Caregiver story

*This is a composite story. It is not a real, identifiable person, but it is built from experiences that many caregivers will recognise.*

Do you remember Anita, from week 1? The mum of three whose Tuesday we followed: the 6am medications drawn up to the millilitre, the twenty-minute hold with the hospital, the forty decisions before mid-morning, the watching that never switches off.

It is eight weeks later, and here is the honest version of what has changed, which is to say: not the Tuesday. Leo's medications still start at six. The NDIS budget is still tight. Last month there was a four-day hospital stay that swallowed a week, the way they do.

But some small things are different. At red lights, more often than not, Anita names one thing she handled, a habit that has quietly survived everything, including the hospital week. When someone asks what she did today, she still says "not much", but a small voice inside her now adds, dryly, "except run a small hospital before nine". She has stopped calling that voice big-headed. It is accurate.

The guilt still visits, but she recognises its knock now, and it does not stay as long. She sat with her check-in from eight weeks ago recently and noticed that two areas felt a little less stretched, one felt the same, and one, money, actually felt harder, because life had kept happening. Her first instinct was to call that a mixed result. Her second, better instinct was this: eight weeks ago she would not have known how to name any of it. Now she can look at the whole picture of her own wellbeing, steadily, without flinching. That is not a mixed result. That is a different relationship with herself.

## 3. Evidence-informed learning

Here is the last piece of learning, and it is about how change actually looks in a life like yours.

**Small and uneven is what real change looks like.** Research on wellbeing in carers does not describe transformations. It describes modest, meaningful shifts: a little more self-understanding, a little less isolation, a harsh inner voice softened, one or two practices that survive contact with real life. Measured against a makeover, that can sound like little. Measured against the actual conditions of caregiving, unrelenting load, chronic interruption, years of accumulated tiredness, it is substantial. If parts of your check-in this week look the same as week 1, or harder, that is not evidence the eight weeks did nothing. Wellbeing in a caring life is tended, not solved, and you now have tools for the tending.

**The road ahead has seasons, and naming that helps.** Research and lived experience both say that caregiving changes shape over time: new stages of your child's life, changes in your own capacity, transitions in schooling, services and eventually adulthood, each bringing its own load. This is not said to daunt you. It is said because caregivers consistently report that knowing the road has seasons, and that ebbing and flowing is normal, makes the hard stretches easier to ride out. What you built here is not a one-off fix; it is a way of returning to yourself that you can use in any season.

**Support is not a phase you graduate from.** Carer Gateway (1800 422 737) remains there for counselling, peer support and respite, whatever week of whatever year it is. Peer networks keep mattering. And some caregivers, once they have a little more room, find real meaning in advocacy or supporting newer families; if that ever calls to you, it is a genuine path. But it is one option, never an obligation. Rest is an equally honourable use of any room you gain.

## 4. Guided reflection

A last set of questions, for looking back and choosing forward. Take your time with them, or take one.

1. **What is one thing you understand about yourself now that you did not have words for eight weeks ago?** It might be about the invisible load, the guilt, the grief, or simply how tired you actually were.

2. **What is the smallest thing that helped the most?** Not the most impressive thing. The one that actually fit your life.

3. **When you speak to yourself now, on a hard day, has the voice changed at all?** Even one degree of gentleness counts, and if it has not changed yet, noticing the voice is itself the beginning.

4. **What would you say to a caregiver starting week 1 today?** Notice that whatever you would offer them, warmth, honesty, permission to do less, is available to you as well, from you, from now on.

## 5. One small step

Here is your final Tiny Step, and it is about the road ahead.

**Before sleep tonight, choose the one small thing you will carry forward.**

As your head hits the pillow, ask yourself: of everything from these eight weeks, which one small thing comes with me? The red-light noticing, the three breaths, the kettle text, the three true words, the one question at the top of the page. Choose one. Not the whole set; one is the right number, because one survives.

That is the whole step, and in a sense it is the whole programme: one small, kind practice, chosen by you, that fits the life you actually have.

## 6. Wellbeing Journey check-in

This week's check-in is the one the whole programme has been quietly walking towards: the same gentle look you took in week 1, taken again, and set alongside it.

Go through the eight areas as you did at the start, noticing whether each feels steady, stretched, or somewhere in between right now:

- **Social:** your connections with other people.
- **Occupational:** your sense of purpose, work or meaningful role, including caregiving itself.
- **Environmental:** the spaces around you and how they feel.
- **Intellectual:** using and feeding your own mind.
- **Spiritual:** meaning, values, or whatever gives you a sense of something larger.
- **Emotional:** how you are feeling, and how much room there is for your feelings.
- **Physical:** your body, rest and energy.
- **Financial:** money, and the stress or steadiness around it.

If you made a note in week 1, set the two side by side. If you are using the Wellbeing Journey app, this is the week it invites you to take the reflection again and shows you the two pictures together, in words, not scores.

Read the comparison the way you would read a friend's: with warmth and without a red pen. Some areas softer, some unchanged, some harder because life kept happening. All of it is information, none of it is a grade, and the fact that you can now see your own wellbeing clearly enough to compare at all is itself one of the quiet gains of these eight weeks.

## 7. Looking ahead

That is Week 8, and that is the programme. Thank you, sincerely, for giving any of your scarce time to this, and for giving it to yourself.

There is no week 9, but nothing here expires. Every week of this course stays open to you: come back to Week 3 when the guilt is loud, Week 6 when things have slipped, any week that once helped. The check-in is yours to take in any season, and Carer Gateway, peer networks and the other supports named along the way are not course features; they are standing parts of the landscape, there whenever you need them.

You arrived eight weeks ago carrying an enormous, mostly invisible load. You are still carrying it, and now a few more people, including you, can see it. Our hope is that you leave with what we promised at the very start: feeling a little more understood, a little less alone, and holding one small thing that is genuinely yours.

Take care of yourself in the small ways you can. You know how now.
