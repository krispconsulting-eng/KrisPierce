# Week 3: The feelings that come with caring

**Theme:** Naming grief, anticipatory grief, guilt and isolation, and finding small anchors of meaning.

*As with every week, there is nothing to complete and nothing to get right. Take what helps and leave the rest.*

---

## 1. Welcome

Welcome back. This week we are going somewhere quieter, and we want to say at the start: you set the pace here. Read as much or as little as you have room for, and step away whenever you need to. All of it will still be here later.

This week is about feelings. Not feelings in general, but the particular ones that come with caring for a child with a rare or serious condition: grief that does not behave the way grief is supposed to, guilt that shows up uninvited, and the loneliness of carrying feelings that are hard to explain to anyone who has not lived them.

Many caregivers have never heard these feelings named out loud. Some have carried them for years believing they were the only one, or that having them meant something was wrong with them. Neither is true, and this week exists to say so.

If it helps to read this with a cup of tea and the door closed, this would be the week for it.

## 2. Caregiver story

*This is a composite story. It is not a real, identifiable person, but it is built from experiences that many caregivers will recognise.*

Erin is mum to Sam, who is six and lives with a rare metabolic condition. Last month she took him to her nephew's birthday party. She wanted to be there. She was glad to be there. And standing at the edge of the backyard, watching a pack of six-year-olds tear around the trampoline while Sam sat with her, she felt something rise in her chest that she could not name in polite company. It was not envy of the other parents, exactly. It was grief, arriving without an invitation, at a party, over sausage rolls.

Then, right behind it, came the guilt. Guilt for feeling sad at a child's birthday. Guilt that Sam might somehow sense it. Guilt, in a quieter voice, about older things: the pregnancy she has combed over a hundred times even though every doctor has told her nothing she did caused this, the therapy session she cancelled once when she was too exhausted to drive, the hour she spends at the gym on Saturdays while her mum sits with Sam. Especially that hour. It is the one hour in her week that is entirely hers, and she spends a good part of it feeling bad about having it.

Erin has never said any of this to anyone. When people ask how she is, she says what most caregivers say. She says she is fine.

If Erin's backyard moment has a version in your life, this week is for you.

## 3. Evidence-informed learning

Here is what is worth knowing, and it may be the most relieving evidence in the whole programme.

**The grief is real, and it has a shape researchers recognise.** The grief that comes with caring for a child with a lifelong condition is not one loss, mourned once. Research describes it as chronic and recurring: it settles for a while, then returns, often set off by ordinary moments, a birthday, a school milestone, a form that asks what your child cannot do. Some researchers call this chronic sorrow. There is also anticipatory grief, felt for losses that have not happened yet but sit on the horizon. Neither means you are stuck or grieving wrongly. This is simply how grief behaves when the situation is ongoing, and it can live alongside deep love and real joy without contradiction.

**Guilt is one of the most common experiences in caregiving.** Studies of parents caring for children with serious and rare conditions repeatedly find guilt woven through the experience: guilt about the diagnosis itself, however irrational; guilt about decisions made with imperfect information; guilt about the needs of other children; and guilt about tending to yourself at all. Knowing guilt is this common does not switch it off, but it changes what it means. Guilt this widespread is not evidence of wrongdoing. It is evidence of caring deeply in an impossible situation.

**Meaning is quietly protective.** Research with parents of children with serious and progressive conditions has found that a strong sense of spirituality or meaning is very common, and is associated with better emotional outcomes [VERIFY: prevalence and outcome findings on spirituality among parents of children with progressive conditions, as cited in the NAC/Global Genes Circle of Care Guidebook]. Spirituality here is broad. For some people it is faith. For others it is nature, music, a sense of purpose, or a few minutes of genuine quiet. The research does not say you must believe anything. It says that having somewhere to stand, something that gives the hardship a place in a larger picture, helps people carry it.

So this week's learning, in one line: the grief is normal, the guilt is common, naming both loosens their grip, and small anchors of meaning are worth keeping.

## 4. Guided reflection

Take these gently, and only the ones that feel safe to sit with today. Nothing needs to be resolved.

1. **Where does grief show up in your life, and does it surprise you?** Like Erin at the party, are there ordinary moments that set it off? What would it be like to greet it as a visitor rather than an intruder?

2. **What does your guilt say, when you let yourself hear it?** Is it about the past, the present, other people, yourself? How much of what it says would you accept if it were said about a friend in your position?

3. **Which feelings do you keep out of view, and who, if anyone, has seen them?** What does it cost to keep saying you are fine?

4. **Where do you find meaning, or where did you once?** Faith, nature, music, quiet, purpose. Is there a small anchor there you could pick up again, even briefly?

If tears come with any of this, they are allowed. Noticing is enough.

## 5. One small step

Here is your Tiny Step for this week. It takes seconds, needs nothing, and works exactly when things are hardest.

**When you notice a harsh thought about yourself, add three true words.**

When you catch the thought, "I should have done more", or "I'm not handling this well", add, silently: "and I'm tired", or "and this is hard". You are not arguing with the thought, and you are not forcing a positive one. You are adding the truth of your circumstances back into the sentence, which is where self-compassion begins.

One time this week is the step done in full. If the harsh thoughts do not come this week, all the better; the step will keep.

## 6. Wellbeing Journey check-in

A brief, kind check-in with the eight areas of the Caregiver Wellbeing Wheel. Nothing is being scored.

This week, you might pay particular attention to two areas:

- **Emotional:** how much room is there for your feelings at the moment? Are they getting named, even privately, or mostly held down? Does this area feel steady, stretched, or somewhere in between?
- **Spiritual:** is there anything in your week that gives you a sense of meaning, or of something larger than the day's demands? Even a small anchor counts.

Give the other six a passing glance if you have room: Social, Occupational, Environmental, Intellectual, Physical, Financial. There is nothing to fix. Noticing where you are is the whole of the check-in, and if this week's noticing felt heavier than last week's, that is not a step backwards. Some weeks simply weigh more.
