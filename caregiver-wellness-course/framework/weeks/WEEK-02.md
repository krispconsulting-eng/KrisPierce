# Week 2: A body that never clocks off

**Theme:** Physical health when there is no spare time, your home as a place that restores you, and what respite can really look like.

## 2. Caregiver story

*This is a composite story. It is not a real, identifiable person, but it is built from experiences that many caregivers will recognise.*

Priya is mum to Ava, who is eight and lives with a rare neuromuscular condition. Ava is fed overnight through a pump, and the pump has opinions. It alarms when the line kinks, when the bag runs low, and sometimes, it seems, for the pleasure of it. Priya has not slept through a night in six years. She no longer describes herself as tired. Tired is the water she swims in.

Her house tells the same story. The hallway holds boxes of feed and syringes because the cupboard filled up years ago. The dining table is where the paperwork lives. The one armchair Priya used to read in is currently home to a folder of NDIS documents and a suction kit. There is no room in the house that belongs to her, and no surface that is not doing a job.

Last month, Priya took Ava to the GP, the paediatrician, the physio and the dietitian. In the waiting room one afternoon, filling in yet another form about Ava's health, she realised she could not remember the last time she had seen a doctor about her own. There was a referral for herself somewhere, expired now. It was not that she did not care. It was that her body had quietly gone to the bottom of a very long list, and nothing ever rose from the bottom of that list.

If some version of this is true for you, you are in familiar company.

## 3. Evidence-informed learning

Here is what is worth knowing this week.

Caring is physical work, and it takes a physical toll. Research on family carers consistently finds that long-term, intensive caring is associated with poorer physical health, disrupted sleep, and less attention to the carer's own medical needs; carers often delay or skip their own appointments while faithfully keeping everyone else's [VERIFY: current AIHW and Carer Wellbeing Survey findings on carer physical health, sleep and preventive care]. Broken sleep, in particular, is one of the heaviest and least visible parts of caring for a child with complex medical needs, and its effects build over years, not days.

Two ideas from the research are genuinely useful here, and neither asks you to find hours you do not have.

The first is that **rest counts in small pieces.** Respite is usually pictured as someone else taking over for a weekend, and for many families that is rare or impossible. But research on carer wellbeing suggests that brief, regular breaks help carers recover and keep going, not only long ones [VERIFY: evidence on brief and regular respite and carer wellbeing outcomes]. Ten minutes where nobody needs you is not a token version of rest. It is rest. In Australia, Carer Gateway (1800 422 737) is a real starting point for respite options, including emergency respite, if that door is one you want to look at some time.

The second is that **your surroundings do some of the work.** A home that is entirely given over to equipment, supplies and paperwork tells you, all day, that you are on duty. Even one small space that is yours, a chair, a corner, one end of a bench, can quietly tell you something different. This is not about a tidy house. It is about having somewhere your eyes can rest.

If your body is worn and your home feels like a ward some days, that is not a sign you have let things go. It is the predictable shape of the load you carry. It makes sense, and small things help more than they should.

## 4. Guided reflection

There are no right answers here, and nothing needs to be written down unless you want to. Take the questions that speak to you and leave the rest.

1. **What is your body carrying right now?** Not what you think you should feel, but what is actually there: the tiredness, the tension, the aches you have stopped mentioning. If your body could file a report, what would be on it?

2. **When did you last drop to the bottom of your own list?** Like Priya's expired referral, is there something of yours, an appointment, a check-up, a sleep debt, that has been quietly waiting for years?

3. **Is there any space in your home, however small, that feels like yours?** If yes, when did you last sit in it? If no, where could one small corner begin?

4. **What does rest actually look like for you, in the life you have now?** Not ideal rest. Real rest, in the sizes that are genuinely available: ten minutes, one cup of tea, one stretch of quiet.

Noticing is enough. Nothing has to change this week for the week to have done its work.

## 5. One small step

Here is your Tiny Step for this week. It takes under a minute, needs nothing, and works on the hardest day.

**While you wait for the kettle, the microwave or the toaster, take three slow breaths.**

Next time you are standing in the kitchen waiting for something to finish, take three breaths, letting each one out a little more slowly than you drew it in. That is the whole step. Your nervous system responds to a longer out-breath even when nothing else in your day slows down.

This is not a breathing programme, and there is nothing to build up to. Three breaths, once, at some point this week, is the step done in full. If you forget, the kettle will boil again tomorrow, and the step will still be here.

## 6. Wellbeing Journey check-in

A brief check-in with the eight areas of the Caregiver Wellbeing Wheel. This is not a test, and nothing is being measured. It is a way of noticing where you are.

This week, you might pay particular attention to two areas:

- **Physical:** how is your body, honestly? Your sleep, your energy, the state of your own health admin. Does this area feel steady, stretched, or somewhere in between?
- **Environmental:** how do the spaces around you feel? Is there anywhere in your home that restores you, even slightly, or is every space on duty?

The other six areas are still there: Social, Occupational, Intellectual, Spiritual, Emotional, Financial. You might give them a passing glance, kindly. If something has shifted since last week, in either direction, that is worth a quiet note. If nothing has, that is a completely normal answer too.
