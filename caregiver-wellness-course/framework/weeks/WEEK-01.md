# Week 1: Where you are right now

**Theme:** Orientation, the reality of an invisible workload, and your first Wellbeing Journey check-in.

*This week is the template for weeks 2 to 8. It follows the framework's 7-part session structure. There is nothing to complete and nothing to get right. Take what helps and leave the rest.*

---

## 1. Welcome

Welcome. We are glad you are here, and we know that being here at all, when your days are so full, is not a small thing.

Before we begin, one promise: this is not a course that is going to ask you to become a better caregiver. You are already doing work that most people will never see or understand. Nothing here is a test, nothing is graded, and there is no way to fall behind.

This first week is gentle on purpose. We are not going to ask much of you. We are going to help you notice where you are right now, name some of what you are carrying, and offer you one small thing to hold on to. That is all.

You can read this in one sitting, or a paragraph at a time between everything else. Two minutes is enough. If you only read this welcome and nothing else this week, that still counts as being here.

This week we are looking at something that shapes almost every caregiver's wellbeing and is almost never named out loud: the invisible workload. Let us start where these things are best started, with someone else's story.

## 2. Caregiver story

*This is a composite story. It is not a real, identifiable person, but it is built from experiences that many caregivers will recognise.*

Anita is a mum of three. Her middle child, Leo, is four, and lives with a developmental and epileptic encephalopathy, a rare condition that causes frequent seizures and affects almost every part of his development. When people ask her what she does, she never quite knows how to answer.

Here is a Tuesday. She is up before six to give Leo his first medications, drawn up to the millilitre, because a missed dose or a late one can mean a bad day of seizures. She checks the overnight notes from Leo's night carer, on the two nights a week they have one. She gets her older child ready for school and settles the baby. Between all of this she rings the hospital to chase a neurology appointment that was meant to come through weeks ago, holds for twenty minutes, and is told to ring a different number. She refills a script that is about to run out. She emails Leo's therapist. She checks Leo's NDIS plan because a support worker's hours are about to lapse and she is not sure the budget will stretch. She watches Leo constantly, the way you watch a child who might seize at any moment, a watching that never fully switches off, even when she is doing something else.

By mid-morning she has made perhaps forty decisions, several of them the kind that most people would find frightening, and she has not sat down.

If you asked Anita at the end of that day what she did, she might say, "Not much. Just the usual." She would not mention the medication timing, the phone calls, the constant watching, the planning three steps ahead for a seizure that might or might not come. To her, it is not work. It is just her life, and it is invisible, even to herself.

Maybe some of this is familiar to you. Maybe your version looks different, but the shape of it, the sheer amount you carry that no one sees, is the same. If so, you are exactly who this week is for.

## 3. Evidence-informed learning

Let us name what Anita is carrying, because naming it is the first relief.

Researchers have a term for this: the invisible workload of caregiving. It is not only the visible, physical tasks of care, the medications, the appointments, the hands-on help. It is also the constant, unseen mental and emotional load: the planning, the anticipating, the remembering, the worrying, the vigilance, the coordinating of a whole system of services around one child. Much of this work happens in your head, all day and often through the night, and precisely because it is invisible, it is rarely recognised, including by the person doing it.

In Australia, the scale of family caregiving is significant. The **ABS Survey of Disability, Ageing and Carers** counts a large number of Australians in caring roles, and shows that a substantial share are primary carers, often women, often caring for many hours every week [VERIFY: latest number and proportion of carers, and primary carer figures, from ABS Survey of Disability, Ageing and Carers]. The **Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW)** reports on the wellbeing of these carers and consistently shows that caring, especially intensive and long-term caring, is associated with poorer wellbeing and higher psychological distress than in the general population [VERIFY: current AIHW finding on carer wellbeing and psychological distress].

The **Carer Wellbeing Survey**, run by Carers Australia and partners, has found that the wellbeing of unpaid carers in Australia sits well below that of the general community, with many carers reporting loneliness and financial strain [VERIFY: current Carer Wellbeing Survey figures on carer wellbeing, loneliness and financial stress]. Parents caring for children with disability, complex medical needs and rare diseases often carry an especially heavy version of this load, sustained over many years, frequently with disrupted sleep and little respite.

Here is the part that matters most for this week. If your wellbeing has taken a hit, that is not a personal weakness, and it is not because you are not coping well enough. It is a predictable, well-documented response to an enormous and largely unseen load carried over a long time. What you are experiencing makes sense. Anyone carrying what you carry would feel it.

That is the whole of the learning this week. Not a strategy, not a fix. This: the load is real, it is heavy, it is mostly invisible, and the toll it takes on you is understandable and shared by many.

## 4. Guided reflection

There are no right answers here, and you do not have to write anything down unless you want to. You might let these questions sit with you while you do something else. Take the ones that speak to you and leave the rest.

1. **When you read Anita's Tuesday, what felt familiar?** Not the details, but the feeling of it. Was there a moment where you thought, "Yes, that is my life too"?

2. **What do you carry that no one sees?** If someone followed you through a day, what would they still miss? Think of the watching, the planning, the worrying, the remembering. What is on your invisible list?

3. **When someone asks what you did today, what do you leave out?** Like Anita saying "not much", what do you quietly not count as work, even though it took real effort and skill?

4. **If a friend were carrying everything you carry, what would you say to them?** Notice whether you would speak to them more gently than you speak to yourself.

You do not need to resolve any of this. Noticing is enough for this week.

## 5. One small step

Here is your Tiny Step for this week. It takes under a minute, it needs nothing, and it works even on a hard day.

**At one red light, or one moment of waiting today, name one thing you handled.**

The next time you are stopped at a red light, waiting in the pick-up line, or standing while a kettle boils, bring to mind one single thing you managed today. A phone call you made. A dose you got right. A hard moment you got through. Name it to yourself, silently.

This is not a to-do list, and it is not about being productive. It is a small act of noticing the work you are already doing, work that so often goes unseen, even by you.

If you forget, that is fine. There will be another red light, and another day. There is nothing to keep up and nothing to break. One time this week is plenty. If it does not suit you, you are welcome to leave it.

## 6. Wellbeing Journey check-in

The Caregiver Wellbeing Wheel has eight parts. This week, we are only taking a first, gentle look. This is not a test, and there are no good or bad scores. It is a way of noticing where you are right now, so that later you can notice what changes.

Take the eight areas below. For each one, you might notice whether it feels steady, stretched, or somewhere in between for you at the moment. There is no need to write anything, though you can if it helps. Go with your first sense of it, and be honest and kind with yourself.

- **Social:** your connections with other people.
- **Occupational:** your sense of purpose, work or meaningful role, including caregiving itself.
- **Environmental:** the spaces around you and how they feel.
- **Intellectual:** using and feeding your own mind.
- **Spiritual:** meaning, values, or whatever gives you a sense of something larger.
- **Emotional:** how you are feeling, and how much room there is for your feelings.
- **Physical:** your body, rest and energy.
- **Financial:** money, and the stress or steadiness around it.

You might notice that some areas feel more stretched than others. That is completely normal, and it is exactly the kind of thing worth knowing. You do not need to do anything about any of it this week. Noticing where you are is the whole of the check-in.

If it would help, you might make a light note of where things sit for you today, somewhere you can find it again in eight weeks. Seeing where you started can be quietly encouraging later on.
