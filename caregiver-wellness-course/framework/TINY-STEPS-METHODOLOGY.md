# Tiny Steps Methodology

The behaviour change approach at the heart of the Caregiver Wellbeing Framework.

---

## Why this exists

Most wellbeing advice was not written for someone whose week can be upended by a seizure, a hospital admission, a missed medication, or a night with no sleep. It assumes a stable, predictable life with spare hours in it. For parents and family caregivers of children with lifelong disability, complex medical needs and rare diseases, that assumption is plainly wrong, and building on a wrong assumption sets people up to feel like they failed.

Tiny Steps is our answer. It is a way of making change that fits inside a real caregiving life, and that keeps working when things get hard.

---

## The principle

**Sustainable change comes from tiny actions that survive a hard week.**

A Tiny Step is a single, specific action that takes under five minutes and can be done even on a bad day. Not on a good day, when there is time and energy to spare, but on an ordinary hard day, which for many caregivers is most days.

The size is the point. We are not starting small so we can build up to something big. Small is the destination, not the runway. A tiny action done most weeks does far more for a caregiver's wellbeing than a large plan that collapses the first time life gets in the way.

We deliberately reject the language of transformation. No overhauls. No "new you". No before and after. Small, real things, tended over time.

---

## How it is grounded in the evidence

Tiny Steps draws on several well-established strands of behaviour change research, translated into plain, caregiver-friendly practice. We name the ideas here so the approach can be trusted and evaluated; caregivers themselves never need to see the theory.

- **Tiny habits.** Behaviour change research consistently shows that very small behaviours, made easy and attached to something you already do, are far more likely to stick than large ambitious ones. Reducing the effort required to start is one of the strongest levers we have.
- **Implementation intentions.** Studies across many settings show that deciding in advance when and where you will do something (an "if this happens, then I will do that" plan) markedly increases follow-through, because it removes the moment-to-moment decision. We build this into how every Tiny Step is worded.
- **Self-compassion.** A growing body of research links self-compassion to lower caregiver distress and greater resilience. Change that is built on kindness is more durable than change built on self-criticism, which tends to produce a cycle of shame and giving up. Tiny Steps are designed to be forgiving on purpose.
- **Reducing decision fatigue.** Caregivers make an enormous number of decisions every day, many of them high stakes. Decision fatigue is real and depleting. By making the next step small, specific and obvious, we ask almost nothing of an already overloaded mind.

Where an organisation wishes to cite specific findings, name the source body and describe the finding qualitatively, and flag any precise figure for human verification, for example `[VERIFY: effect size / finding from ___ systematic review]`, in keeping with the framework's evidence standards.

## How a Tiny Step is designed

Every Tiny Step must meet these tests before it is offered to a caregiver:

- **Specific.** Not "rest more", but a single clear action the caregiver can picture doing. Vague intentions do not survive contact with a hard day.
- **Achievable during a crisis week.** The real test is not "could I do this on a calm Sunday?" but "could I do this in the middle of a hospital admission, on very little sleep?" If the answer is no, the step is too big.
- **Tied to an existing routine.** We attach the step to something the caregiver already does without thinking: the kettle boiling, buckling the car seat, waiting for a medication to draw up, the moment before sleep. The existing habit becomes the reminder, so nothing new has to be remembered.
- **No equipment.** No apps to download, nothing to buy, nothing to set up. If it needs a purchase or a device, it is not a Tiny Step.
- **Forgiving.** It works if it is done for ten seconds instead of a minute. It works if it is missed for a week. There is no streak to break and nothing to keep up.

If a step fails any of these tests, it is reworked or set aside. We would rather offer one genuinely tiny step than a well-meaning one that quietly asks too much.

---

## How setbacks are handled

This is where Tiny Steps differs most sharply from ordinary self-improvement advice.

**Every week is a fresh start. There is no guilt, and no penalty.**

A missed step is not a failure. It is information: the week was hard, and the caregiver was doing what the week demanded. That is not a lapse in discipline; it is a reasonable response to a difficult reality.

- **No streaks.** We never count consecutive days or weeks, because streaks turn a missed day into a loss, and caregivers already carry enough loss.
- **No catching up.** There is nothing to make up for. You do not owe last week anything.
- **No judgement, in the wording or the design.** We never say "you fell behind" or "get back on track". We say, in effect: this week is new, and you can begin again whenever you have room.
- **Setbacks are normalised out loud.** We tell caregivers, plainly and often, that missing steps is expected and normal. Naming it removes its sting.

The measure of success is never consistency. It is whether, over time, the caregiver feels a little more understood, a little more confident, and able to return to a small kind action when they have the room for it.

## Worked examples

Each example is mapped to one dimension of the Caregiver Wellbeing Wheel. Each is specific, tied to an existing routine, needs no equipment, takes under five minutes, and survives a hard week. Notice that none of them ask the caregiver to find extra time; each borrows a moment that already exists.

### 1. Social
**While the kettle boils, send one text.** When you next make a hot drink, send a single message to one person, even three words. No need for a conversation, no need to explain how you are. The kettle is the reminder; when it clicks off, the step is done. If you do not feel up to it, the kettle will boil again tomorrow.

### 2. Occupational
**At one red light, name one thing you handled.** Caregiving is skilled, relentless work that mostly goes unseen, including by the person doing it. Next time you are stopped at a red light or waiting in the pick-up line, name to yourself one thing you managed today, however small. This is not a to-do list. It is noticing the work you are already doing.

### 3. Environmental
**When you fill your child's water, clear one thing near you.** Your surroundings shape how you feel, and caregiving homes fill up fast with equipment, paperwork and supplies. When you next fill a drink bottle or a cup, move one single item to where it belongs, or into the bin. One item, not the bench. The point is a small sense of order, not a tidy house.

### 4. Intellectual
**While a medication draws up or a machine runs, read one paragraph.** Many caregivers miss using their minds for anything other than care. When you are waiting for a pump, a feed, a nebuliser or a medication, read one paragraph of something that has nothing to do with your child's condition. One paragraph is enough. It is a small reminder that your mind is still yours.

### 5. Spiritual
**Before sleep, name one thing that mattered today.** As your head hits the pillow, bring to mind one moment that felt meaningful, or quietly true: a look from your child, a quiet minute, a small kindness. Not gratitude on demand, and not looking for a silver lining. Noticing one thing that mattered. If nothing comes, that is a fair answer on a hard day.

### 6. Emotional
**When you buckle the car seat, name how you feel in one word.** Caregivers often lose track of their own feelings because there is no time to check. As you clip the seatbelt or the harness, silently name your own feeling in a single word: tired, flat, tense, okay. You do not have to do anything about it. Naming it is the whole step, and it quietly loosens its hold.

### 7. Physical
**While you wait for the microwave, take three slow breaths.** Not a breathing programme, not ten minutes of stillness you do not have. When you are standing at the microwave, the kettle or the toaster, take three slow breaths, letting each one out a little longer than you drew it in. Your nervous system responds to this even when your day does not slow down.

### 8. Financial
**On one bill or letter, write the single next action, then put it down.** Money and admin are a heavy, constant strain for many caregiving families. Next time a bill or an official letter arrives, do not try to solve it. Write on a sticky note or the envelope the one next thing to do with it (ring this number, ask this question, file it), then set it down. You have made it smaller and taken the weight of holding it in your head.

### 9. Emotional / self-compassion (bonus)
**When you notice a harsh thought about yourself, add three words.** When you catch yourself thinking "I should have" or "I'm not doing enough", add, silently, "and I'm tired", or "and this is hard". You are not arguing with the thought or forcing a positive one. You are adding the truth of your circumstances back in, which is where self-compassion begins.

### 10. Social / connection (bonus)
**When you see another parent waiting, offer one small nod.** In a clinic, a waiting room or a school pick-up, when you notice another caregiver who looks like they are carrying a lot, offer one small gesture: a nod, a half-smile, holding a door. Connection does not have to be a conversation. A moment of being seen goes both ways.

## Using Tiny Steps across the programme

Within the eight-week guided programme, each week offers one Tiny Step tied to that week's theme and to a Wellness Wheel dimension. Caregivers are always free to keep an earlier step, swap it, or set it down. The steps are not cumulative and are not tracked against each other. A caregiver who carries one single Tiny Step through the whole eight weeks has done this well.

The goal is never a full wheel or a perfect record. The goal is that tending your own wellbeing starts to feel possible, in the life you actually have.
